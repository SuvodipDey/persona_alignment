OPINION QA REPRESENTATIVENESS ANALYSIS
=======================================
Metric  : Rep(g) = 1 - (1/|Q|) * sum_q TVD( P^G(q) || P_hat^G(q) )
          where TVD is the Total Variation Distance between human and model
          response distributions per question q in persona group g
Approach: Baseline (no context) vs. RAG-augmented model (semantically similar
          question-answer pairs as in-context examples via SentenceTransformer
          retrieval per persona vector DB)
Dataset : 40 personas, ~8,357 test question-answer pairs

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
OVERALL SUMMARY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mean Rep(g) (baseline) : 0.4827
Mean Rep(g) (model)    : 0.4935
Overall improvement    : +2.24%
Personas improved      : 25 / 40  (62.5%)
Personas degraded      : 14 / 40  (35.0%)
Near-zero change (±1%) :  7 / 40  (17.5%)

The RAG improvement on Opinion QA is substantially smaller than on the other
two tasks (Morale Machine: +7.77%, Website AES: +22.76%). The improvement
distribution is tightly concentrated: only 5 personas improve by more than
10%, while most changes fall within ±5%. This reflects a fundamental
difference in task structure that limits RAG's effectiveness.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. WHY OPINION QA IS HARDER: TASK STRUCTURE DIFFERENCES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Unlike Moral Machine (7 scenario types, binary choice) and Website AES
(visual ratings, single scale), Opinion QA spans 200+ distinct survey
questions across completely unrelated domains: gun control, abortion,
immigration, racial attitudes, climate science, gender roles, AI, healthcare.

This creates two fundamental limitations for RAG:

  a) LOW CROSS-TOPIC TRANSFERABILITY: A persona's opinion on immigration
     does not reliably predict their opinion on AI regulation, even if the
     questions are semantically similar by surface text. The retrieved "similar"
     questions may come from a different ideological dimension than the one
     being queried, carrying the wrong distributional signal.

  b) HIGH WITHIN-PERSONA VARIANCE: Each persona is described by a few
     political/social positions, but must answer questions on dozens of topics
     that the description never explicitly addresses. Without examples, the
     model guesses; with examples, it may latch onto an adjacent but incorrect
     pattern from a retrieved question.

  c) TVD IS SENSITIVE TO OPTION-LEVEL DISTRIBUTION: The metric captures whether
     the full probability distribution over all options matches, not just the
     modal answer. Even if the RAG examples push the model toward the right
     modal choice, they may shift probability mass away from secondary options
     in the human distribution, worsening TVD without changing accuracy.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2. WHY SOME PERSONAS ARE WELL-ALIGNED AT BASELINE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Personas with the highest baseline Rep(g) (> 0.52) are:

  - Persona 15 (Asian woman, Seattle, environmental scientist, liberal) : 0.543
  - Persona 5  (Asian man, TX, Korean-born engineer, very conservative) : 0.535
  - Persona 6  (Black man, rural GA, law enforcement, very conservative): 0.535
  - Persona 14 (White man, SF, tech executive, very liberal)            : 0.529
  - Persona 4  (White woman, IN, homeschooling mother, Mormon)          : 0.525
  - Persona 2  (White woman, rural MS, nurse's aide, Evangelical)       : 0.524
  - Persona 17 (White woman, Vermont, retired professor, very liberal)  : 0.526

Key observations:

  a) STRONG IDEOLOGICAL ANCHORING: The best-aligned personas have clearly
     defined, publicly legible ideological profiles. A tech executive from San
     Francisco, an environmental scientist from Seattle, or an Evangelical from
     rural Mississippi each map onto well-documented opinion clusters that the
     LLM has learned from survey commentary and political discourse in pretraining.
     The model does not need examples to infer that a Seattle environmentalist
     supports climate policy or that an Evangelical opposes abortion.

  b) DEMOGRAPHICALLY REPRESENTATIVE ARCHETYPES: Personas 14 (tech liberal) and
     6 (law enforcement conservative) represent common demographic archetypes
     well-represented in political surveys and news coverage. The LLM's priors
     for these profiles are already well-calibrated.

  c) STRONG SINGLE-ISSUE COHERENCE: Personas like 5 and 6, despite their
     unusual demographic combinations, have one dominating worldview axis
     (free-market conservatism, law-and-order) that generalises across many
     question topics, enabling consistent zero-shot predictions.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3. WHY SOME PERSONAS ARE POORLY ALIGNED AT BASELINE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Personas with the lowest baseline Rep(g) (< 0.46) are:

  - Persona 3  (Hispanic man, TX, border patrol, shifted conservative) : 0.396
  - Persona 36 (Asian man, Seattle, data scientist, independent)       : 0.438
  - Persona 8  (Young White man, Alabama, very conservative)           : 0.436
  - Persona 13 (Hispanic man, LA, barista/activist, very liberal)      : 0.446
  - Persona 18 (Black man, Philadelphia, community organiser, liberal) : 0.458

Key observations:

  a) "SHIFTED" OR UNCONVENTIONAL POLITICAL PROFILES: Persona 3 (Hispanic border
     patrol officer who has "shifted" to very conservative) and persona 8
     (young rural conservative) represent profiles where the demographic and
     ideological combination is counter-intuitive. The model may default to
     the demographic prior (young or Hispanic → more moderate/liberal) and
     underweight the declared ideology, producing misaligned responses.

  b) IDEOLOGICALLY INDEPENDENT PERSONAS: Persona 36 (data scientist, independent,
     "applies data-driven reasoning to every problem") has no stable party
     alignment the model can use as a shortcut. Each question requires inferring
     the persona's position from first principles, which is highly uncertain
     without examples.

  c) ACTIVIST PERSONAS WITH VERY SPECIFIC VIEW CLUSTERS: Personas 13 (prison
     abolition, open borders) and 18 (reparations, police abolition) hold
     positions that sit at the far left of the distribution on specific issues
     but may be indistinguishable from mainstream liberals on many survey
     questions. The model struggles to capture the degree and consistency of
     their radicalism.

4. PERSONAS MOST IMPROVED BY RAG
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Top gains:

  - Persona 16 (White man, Boston, non-profit, very liberal)           : +18.2%
                0.469 → 0.555
  - Persona 36 (Asian man, Seattle, data scientist, independent)       : +16.5%
                0.438 → 0.511
  - Persona 3  (Hispanic man, TX, border patrol, conservative)         : +14.3%
                0.396 → 0.452
  - Persona 24 (Black man, rural MS, conservative Republican legislator: +12.6%
                0.454 → 0.511
  - Persona 30 (Hispanic woman, NM, elderly, Catholic, low-income)     : +11.9%
                0.441 → 0.493
  - Persona 34 (Hispanic woman, Houston, cardiologist, independent)    :  +8.8%
                0.481 → 0.524
  - Persona 37 (Hispanic woman, Phoenix, real estate, moderate)        :  +7.1%
                0.482 → 0.517

Why does RAG help these personas?

  a) LOW-BASELINE PROFILES WITH CONSISTENT IDEOLOGICAL TEXTURE: Personas 3, 24,
     and 30 each have a low baseline but a coherent world-view that, once
     exemplified via a few retrieved Q&A pairs, allows the model to generalise
     correctly. A conservative Hispanic border patrol officer's opinions on
     immigration-adjacent questions provide a strong anchor from which the
     model can infer consistent stances across other topics (law enforcement,
     social order, border security).

  b) REASONING-STYLE PERSONAS BENEFIT FROM EXAMPLES: Persona 36 (data
     scientist, independent, data-driven) and persona 34 (cardiologist,
     independent, healthcare-focused) have a distinctive cognitive style rather
     than a fixed ideological position. Retrieved examples effectively show
     the model how this style manifests in specific opinion choices, something
     the system prompt alone cannot demonstrate. Both improve strongly.

  c) UNDERREPRESENTED CULTURAL IDENTITIES: Persona 30 (elderly Hispanic
     Catholic farmworker) represents an intersectional profile not well
     represented in mainstream political discourse. The retrieved examples
     from demographically similar training annotators provide essential
     calibration that the LLM lacks from pretraining.

  d) YOUNG PROGRESSIVE MALES: Persona 16 (Boston non-profit worker, very
     liberal) shows the largest absolute gain. Despite a clear left-wing
     profile, the base model likely blunts the sharpness of his opinions
     (moderating toward safer responses). The RAG examples from similar
     left-wing training annotators restore the full distributional radicalism,
     better matching the human labels.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5. PERSONAS MOST DEGRADED BY RAG
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Top regressions:

  - Persona 8  (18-year-old White man, Alabama, very conservative)     : -11.6%
                0.436 → 0.386
  - Persona 21 (White man, GA, small business owner, very conservative): -8.7%
                0.506 → 0.462
  - Persona 5  (Asian man, TX, Korean-born engineer, very conservative): -7.2%
                0.535 → 0.496
  - Persona 27 (Native American woman, SD, progressive Democrat)       : -6.9%
                0.507 → 0.472
  - Persona 35 (White man, Boston, law partner, socially moderate Rep.): -4.7%
                0.485 → 0.462
  - Persona 28 (Hispanic farmworker, CA, low-income Democrat)          : -4.3%
                0.461 → 0.441

Why does RAG hurt these personas?

  a) HIGH-BASELINE PERSONAS DISTURBED BY MISMATCHED EXAMPLES: Persona 5
     (Korean-born conservative engineer) and persona 21 (conservative small
     business owner) both had solid baselines (>0.50). For these personas the
     model already approximates the opinion distribution reasonably well. The
     retrieved similar questions may come from training annotators who share
     the same broad ideology but differ on specific sub-issues — for instance,
     a Korean-American conservative may have views on immigration and trade that
     differ from Anglo-American conservatives. The examples shift the model
     toward the majority-conservative pattern and away from this persona's
     culturally-inflected variant.

  b) PERSONA 8 — MOST SEVERE DEGRADATION: The 18-year-old Alabama conservative
     is the single most degraded persona (-11.6%). This profile is extremely
     sparse in opinion survey datasets: very young (18), very conservative, low
     income, rural. The retrieved "similar" questions from the training DB are
     likely answered by older, more ideologically stable conservatives whose
     distributional responses differ systematically from those of a teenager.
     The RAG examples actively introduce age-driven mismatch, pulling the
     model away from the base model's already-weak but better-calibrated guess.

  c) CROSS-CUTTING PROGRESSIVE IDENTITIES: Persona 27 (Native American
     progressive) and persona 28 (Hispanic farmworker Democrat) both combine
     progressive politics with non-mainstream cultural identities whose opinion
     patterns may not cluster with generic progressive annotators in the
     training set. Retrieved examples from demographically mismatched progressives
     introduce opinion distributions that conflict with the persona's specific
     community-informed views (tribal sovereignty, labour rights vs. generic
     liberal positions).

  d) SOCIALLY MODERATE CONSERVATIVES REGRESS: Persona 35 (law firm partner,
     "socially moderate" conservative) and persona 21 (small business owner)
     have cross-cutting profiles where the conservative economic views coexist
     with moderate social views. Retrieved examples from ideologically pure
     conservatives overwrite the social moderation, producing a more extreme
     and less representative distribution.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
6. CROSS-CUTTING PATTERNS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A. Improvement Distribution Is Tightly Bounded
   The largest gain is +18.2% and the worst loss is -11.6%. In contrast,
   Morale Machine ranges from +64% to -27%, and Website AES from +961% to
   -124%. Opinion QA's opinion-by-opinion diversity makes the task inherently
   resistant to large RAG-driven swings in either direction.

B. Very Conservative Personas — More Often Degraded
   Improved: 1, 2, 3, 4, 9, 10, 24 (7 personas)
   Degraded: 5, 6, 7, 8, 21, 22, 35 (7 personas)
   Conservative personas split evenly. Those with strong single-issue anchors
   (border patrol, Southern Baptist) improve; those with high baselines or
   culturally complex conservatism (Korean engineer, Orthodox rabbi, socially
   moderate conservative) degrade. Conservative opinion patterns are more
   heterogeneous across subgroups than the system prompt conveys.

C. Very Liberal Personas — Mostly Improved
   Improved: 11, 16, 17, 18, 19, 20 (6 personas)
   Degraded: 13, 14, 15, 27 (4 personas)
   Liberal improvement is modest but consistent across mainstream liberal
   profiles. The degraded liberal personas are either extreme-left (13, prison
   abolition) or uniquely-positioned (27, Native American progressive). The
   mainstream liberal signal is well-represented in training data, making
   retrieval more useful on average.

D. Moderate and Independent Personas — Mixed but RAG-Friendly
   Personas 31, 34, 36, 37, 38, 39 (moderates/independents) are mostly
   improved. Without a strong ideological prior, the base model defaults to
   bland centrism. RAG provides persona-specific examples that break out of
   this default, revealing genuine moderate preference patterns.

E. The RAG Benefit Correlates Inversely With Baseline Strength
   Across all degraded personas, 4 of the top 5 had baseline Rep(g) > 0.48.
   Across all improved personas, 7 of the top 8 had baseline Rep(g) < 0.48.
   This mirrors the Morale Machine and Website AES findings: the lower the
   starting alignment, the more the model needs calibration and the less it
   has to lose from imperfect examples.

F. Question Volume Per Persona Does Not Drive Outcomes
   n_questions ranges from 163 to 258. There is no clear correlation between
   sample size and RAG benefit, confirming that retrieval quality (not quantity)
   determines improvement.
