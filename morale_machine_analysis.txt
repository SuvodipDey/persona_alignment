MORAL MACHINE ALIGNMENT ANALYSIS
==================================
Metric  : A(g) = 1 - ||h^G - h_hat^G||_2 / sqrt(n)
          where h^G is the vector of human Option-A rates per moral dimension
          and h_hat^G is the corresponding vector of model rates
Approach: Baseline (no context) vs. RAG-augmented model (text-similarity top-k
          scenario examples as in-context demonstrations)
Dataset : 40 personas, ~10,000 test records

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
OVERALL SUMMARY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Mean alignment (baseline) : 0.7408
Mean alignment (model)    : 0.7984
Overall improvement       : +7.77%
Personas improved by RAG  : 29 / 40  (72.5%)
Personas degraded by RAG  : 11 / 40  (27.5%)

The RAG approach is broadly effective for moral decision alignment, with nearly
three-quarters of personas benefiting. However, the impact is highly uneven:
improvements range from +64% to virtually zero, while regressions reach -27%.
This asymmetry signals that the value of retrieved moral examples depends
strongly on persona-specific properties.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. WHY SOME PERSONAS ARE WELL-ALIGNED AT BASELINE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Personas with the highest baseline alignment (>0.84) are:

  - Persona 18 (Black man, Philadelphia, community organiser, very liberal): 0.911
  - Persona 24 (Black man, rural MS, conservative Republican legislator)  : 0.878
  - Persona 13 (Hispanic man, LA, barista/activist, very liberal)         : 0.842
  - Persona 17 (White woman, Vermont, retired professor, very liberal)    : 0.847
  - Persona 20 (Asian woman, Portland, student, very liberal, anti-cap)   : 0.844

Key observations:

  a) IDEOLOGICALLY COHERENT PROFILES: The highest-baseline personas have strong,
     internally consistent value systems that map predictably onto moral
     dimensions. A very liberal activist or a principled conservative legislator
     makes decisions along a stable axis (save more people, prioritise the young,
     or defer to status) that the LLM can infer reliably from the persona
     description alone without examples. The system prompt sufficiently captures
     the moral framework.

  b) BLACK PROGRESSIVE URBAN PERSONAS (18, 24) score near the top for opposite
     political reasons, both benefiting from being culturally well-represented
     in the training corpus of the underlying LLM. The model has strong priors
     for how people from these backgrounds reason about trolley-problem-style
     dilemmas.

  c) YOUNG, POLITICALLY ENGAGED PERSONAS tend to have high baseline alignment.
     Their moral reasoning is shaped by contemporary discourse that is
     well-represented in LLM pretraining data, making zero-shot prediction
     relatively accurate.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2. WHY SOME PERSONAS ARE POORLY ALIGNED AT BASELINE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Personas with the lowest baseline alignment (<0.65) are:

  - Persona 32 (White woman, Midwest, retired nurse, moderate Republican) : 0.500
  - Persona 2  (White woman, rural MS, nurse's aide, Evangelical)         : 0.515
  - Persona 31 (White man, Connecticut, retired judge, moderate Indep.)   : 0.607
  - Persona 23 (Indian American woman, Houston, Hindu pharmacist)         : 0.613

Key observations:

  a) MODERATE AND CROSS-CUTTING PROFILES: Personas 31 and 32 are political
     moderates whose moral reasoning does not follow either ideological pole
     clearly. The model defaults to population-average behaviour, which may
     diverge from a persona whose views blend conservative social instincts
     with pragmatic or procedural moral thinking. Without examples, the LLM
     cannot infer which way the moderate will lean on each dimension.

  b) CULTURALLY-SPECIFIC MORAL FRAMEWORKS: Persona 23 (devout Hindu) and
     persona 2 (Evangelical Protestant) have moral intuitions shaped by
     specific religious traditions that do not map neatly onto the liberal-
     conservative axis the model implicitly uses. The LLM underestimates how
     strongly religious cosmology influences concrete moral choices (e.g.,
     attitude toward fate, animal vs. human life, elder vs. youth).

  c) RURAL, LOW-EDUCATION, WORKING-CLASS PERSONAS: Persona 2 (nurse's aide,
     rural MS) sits at the intersection of several underrepresented demographic
     groups. The model appears to misread this profile, possibly conflating
     "very conservative" with a different cognitive style than the one actually
     demonstrated in the data.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3. PERSONAS MOST IMPROVED BY RAG
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Top gains:

  - Persona 2  (White woman, rural MS, Evangelical, very conservative)   : +64.5%
                0.515 → 0.847
  - Persona 31 (White man, CT, retired judge, moderate Independent)      : +42.7%
                0.607 → 0.866
  - Persona 32 (White woman, Midwest, retired nurse, moderate Rep.)      : +29.3%
                0.500 → 0.646
  - Persona 25 (Black woman, Detroit, Republican precinct captain)       : +28.6%
                0.666 → 0.857
  - Persona 9  (White woman, rural MS, elderly, Southern Baptist)        : +27.9%
                0.665 → 0.850
  - Persona 15 (Asian woman, Seattle, environmental scientist, liberal)  : +22.0%
                0.746 → 0.910
  - Persona 21 (White man, suburban GA, small business owner, conserv.)  : +21.3%
                0.746 → 0.904

Why does RAG help these personas?

  a) CORRECTING SYSTEMATIC MISALIGNMENT IN RELIGIOUS/RURAL CONSERVATIVES:
     Personas 2 and 9 are Evangelical/Baptist women from rural Mississippi with
     minimal education. The base model substantially underestimates how their
     moral choices cluster — they may have strong, consistent rules (e.g., always
     save humans over animals, never sacrifice the elderly) that only become
     apparent through example scenarios. A few retrieved scenarios showing the
     actual choices of similar annotators anchors the model's decision-making
     to that consistent pattern, producing a large jump in alignment.

  b) MODERATES AND INSTITUTIONALISTS BENEFIT ENORMOUSLY: Personas 31 and 32
     (judge, retired nurse, both moderate/centre) had the worst baselines. For
     them, RAG is transformative because example scenarios reveal which specific
     moral intuitions drive their decisions (proceduralism, care ethics, pragmatic
     harm reduction) — things that cannot be inferred from "moderate Independent"
     or "moderate Republican" alone. The examples serve as a moral calibration
     that the system prompt entirely lacks.

  c) UNCONVENTIONAL IDEOLOGICAL COMBINATIONS: Persona 25 (Black Republican
     precinct captain) is a profile the model struggles to represent correctly
     zero-shot, likely reverting to assumptions based on race alone. Retrieved
     scenarios from the corresponding training distribution demonstrate the
     actual moral reasoning pattern (individual responsibility, community
     pragmatism) and correct the LLM's default.

  d) HIGH-BASELINE PERSONAS THAT STILL IMPROVE (15, 21): These personas already
     had reasonable baseline alignment (~0.75) but RAG pushed them toward 0.90+.
     Here the retrieved examples fine-tune the model's response on the specific
     moral dimensions where the persona has idiosyncratic preferences (e.g.,
     a conservative businessman's attitude toward fitness/status vs. numbers),
     resolving ambiguity that the persona description leaves underspecified.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4. PERSONAS MOST DEGRADED BY RAG
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Top regressions:

  - Persona 12 (Black woman, Chicago, civil rights attorney, very liberal): -27.4%
                0.832 → 0.604
  - Persona 10 (White man, rural Nebraska, retired farmer, very conserv.): -13.2%
                0.790 → 0.685
  - Persona 18 (Black man, Philadelphia, community organiser, very lib.) : -12.8%
                0.911 → 0.794
  - Persona 8  (White man, rural Alabama, young, very conservative)      : -11.7%
                0.819 → 0.723
  - Persona 17 (White woman, Vermont, retired professor, very liberal)   :  -4.6%
                0.847 → 0.809

Why does RAG hurt these personas?

  a) HIGH-BASELINE PERSONAS DISRUPTED BY NOISY EXAMPLES: Personas 12, 18, 17,
     and 8 all had strong baseline alignment (≥0.82). For these personas the
     system prompt already captures their moral framework very well. The
     retrieved scenario examples, while textually similar (same moral dimension,
     same option framing), may come from training annotators who matched the
     persona's demographic profile only partially, introducing decision patterns
     that slightly contradict the persona's actual consistency.

     This is the RAG analogue of a "distractor" effect: the model adapts too
     much to the retrieved demonstrations and over-corrects from a good default.

  b) PERSONA 12 — THE MOST STRIKING REGRESSION:
     The civil rights attorney (Black woman, Chicago, very liberal) had the
     third-highest baseline (0.832), suggesting the model already understood
     her moral profile well. The drop to 0.604 is severe. One likely cause:
     the retrieved similar scenarios come from training examples matched by
     Black female annotators generally, but this specific persona has a highly
     principled, rights-based moral framework (she is a lawyer). The training
     examples may skew toward more populist or utilitarian choices, actively
     misleading the model away from her legalistic, principle-first reasoning.
     The RAG examples are demographically adjacent but morally misaligned.

  c) RURAL, ISOLATED PERSONAS (10): Persona 10 (80-year-old Nebraska farmer,
     never left his county) has an extremely idiosyncratic moral worldview rooted
     in traditional rural values. Even scenarios that are textually similar may
     come from different geographic or occupational contexts where the same
     demographic cluster makes systematically different choices. With only a
     small training set for this persona, the retrieved examples may reflect
     noise more than signal.

  d) YOUNG IDEOLOGICALLY PURE PERSONAS (8): The 18-year-old Alabama conservative
     is ideologically coherent but demographically unusual (young, rural, very
     conservative, low income). The base model handles this reasonably well
     (0.819). The retrieved examples may come from older conservative annotators
     with different decision patterns, pulling the model away from the specific
     youthful impulsiveness and military-first logic that characterises this
     persona.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5. CROSS-CUTTING PATTERNS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A. Low Baseline → RAG Consistently Helps
   All four personas with baseline < 0.65 improved with RAG (avg +30%).
   This mirrors the website AES findings: the lower the baseline, the more
   room for improvement, and the more the model needs calibration examples
   to override its misaligned default.

B. Very High Baseline → RAG Often Hurts
   Of the 7 personas with baseline > 0.84, five showed degradation or near-
   zero change. Only personas 15 and 20 improved. This "ceiling disturbance"
   effect — where well-calibrated zero-shot behaviour is disrupted by
   potentially mismatched examples — is the dominant risk of RAG.

C. Very Liberal Personas — Split Outcome
   Improved: 11, 15, 16, 19, 20, 26, 27 (7 personas)
   Degraded: 12, 13, 14, 17, 18 (5 personas)
   The dividing line appears to be baseline alignment: those already above 0.84
   are more likely to degrade. Younger, lower-income liberals benefit more;
   older, higher-income, or more experienced liberals (professor, attorney,
   tech executive) tend to regress, perhaps because their nuanced principled
   frameworks are harder to capture via demographic similarity alone.

D. Very Conservative Personas — Mostly Improved
   Improved: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 9, 21, 22, 24, 25, 26, 28, 30 (14 personas)
   Degraded: 7, 8, 10 (3 personas)
   Conservative personas benefit substantially from RAG. This may reflect that
   conservative moral reasoning in trolley-problem scenarios (often prioritising
   hierarchy, order, numbers) is harder for the LLM to infer zero-shot without
   concrete grounding from actual annotators, making retrieved examples especially
   valuable.

E. Moral Dimensions Drive the Pattern
   The alignment metric measures correlation across 7 moral scenario dimensions
   (Age, Gender, Fitness, Social Value, Species, PedPed, etc.). Personas with
   strong, consistent preferences along specific dimensions (e.g., always save
   pedestrians over passengers, always save more people) align well regardless
   of approach. Personas who apply different weights to different dimensions
   in non-obvious ways are the hardest to calibrate — and benefit most from
   examples that reveal those dimension-specific preferences.

F. Persona 18 Is the Clearest RAG Failure
   With the highest baseline alignment of any persona (0.911), persona 18 had
   the most to lose from bad RAG examples. The -12.8% regression from near-
   perfect alignment to 0.794 demonstrates that RAG can be actively harmful
   when the zero-shot model is already well-calibrated. Applying RAG selectively
   — only when the baseline is below a threshold — would have protected this
   persona's alignment.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
6. COMPARISON WITH WEBSITE AESTHETICS FINDINGS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Both tasks show the same fundamental pattern:

  Same: Low baseline → RAG helps; high baseline → RAG risks degradation.
  Same: Unconventional ideological combinations benefit most from RAG.
  Same: Religious/culturally-specific moral frameworks are hard to capture
        without examples.

  Different: Moral decisions are more globally consistent per persona than
             aesthetic ratings. A persona's moral intuitions tend to generalise
             across scenario types, so a single well-matched example can
             recalibrate the entire decision axis. In contrast, aesthetic
             preferences are more image-specific and noisier, requiring
             more examples to achieve a comparable relative improvement.
  Different: The overall RAG improvement is smaller for Moral Machine (+7.77%)
             than for Website AES (+22.76%), but the baseline is already higher
             (0.74 vs. 0.20), leaving less room for gain.
