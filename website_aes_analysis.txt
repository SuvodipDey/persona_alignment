WEBSITE AESTHETICS ALIGNMENT ANALYSIS
======================================
Metric  : Pearson correlation between model rating and human mean_response, per persona
Approach: Baseline (no context) vs. RAG-augmented model (CLIP top-k similar images as in-context examples)
Dataset : 40 personas, ~10,000 test records, ~86-96 unique websites per persona

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
OVERALL SUMMARY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mean Pearson r (baseline) : 0.2016
Mean Pearson r (model)    : 0.2474
Overall improvement       : +22.76%
Personas improved by RAG  : 28 / 40  (70%)
Personas degraded by RAG  : 12 / 40  (30%)

The RAG approach is broadly beneficial but far from universal. A substantial
minority of personas are hurt, indicating that visual similarity retrieval is
not the right signal for every demographic group.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. WHY SOME PERSONAS ARE WELL-ALIGNED (BASELINE)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The personas with the highest baseline correlations (>0.30) are:

  - Persona 38 (Black woman, Houston, social media manager, moderate Democrat): 0.401
  - Persona 26 (Somali-American woman, Minneapolis, Muslim moderate)      : 0.386
  - Persona 16 (White man, Boston, non-profit worker, very liberal)       : 0.337
  - Persona 8  (Young White man, rural Alabama, very conservative)        : 0.335
  - Persona 14 (White man, SF, tech executive, very liberal)              : 0.330
  - Persona 23 (Indian American woman, Houston, Hindu, conservative)      : 0.314
  - Persona 37 (Hispanic woman, Phoenix, real estate, moderate)           : 0.302

Key observations about high-baseline personas:
  a) Several are middle-income, educated, urban or suburban — demographic
     groups that likely constitute the majority of human raters in the original
     dataset. The model has implicitly learned aesthetic preferences that
     correlate well with these mainstream profiles without any examples.
  b) Persona 8 (rural Alabama conservative) is an outlier in this group —
     its relatively strong baseline may reflect that young men from any
     ideology share certain aesthetic preferences (clean design, bold visuals)
     that the model already captures from general training.
  c) Social-media-native personas (persona 38) tend to have more consistent,
     contemporary aesthetic preferences that map cleanly onto visual features
     (color vibrancy, modern layout), making them easier for a vision model
     to predict correctly without examples.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2. PERSONAS MOST IMPROVED BY RAG
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Top gains:

  - Persona 6  (Black man, rural GA, law enforcement, very conservative): +961%
                0.021 → 0.222
  - Persona 27 (Native American woman, SD, progressive)                 : +231%
                0.141 → 0.467
  - Persona 19 (Hispanic woman, Denver, teacher, very liberal)          : +143%
                0.159 → 0.387
  - Persona 25 (Black woman, Detroit, Republican precinct captain)      : +137%
                0.042 → 0.100
  - Persona 22 (Jewish man, NYC, Orthodox rabbi, conservative)          : +132%
                0.102 → 0.237
  - Persona 15 (Asian woman, Seattle, environmental scientist, liberal) : +116%
                0.152 → 0.328
  - Persona 11 (White woman, Brooklyn, social work, very liberal)       :  +80%
                0.133 → 0.240

Common pattern: ALL top-improving personas started with very low baseline
correlations (r < 0.17). This is the dominant signal for RAG benefit.

Why does RAG help these personas so much?

  a) UNCONVENTIONAL PROFILES: Personas 6, 25, 27 combine demographic attributes
     that are statistically rare in human rating pools (Black conservative,
     Native American progressive, Black Republican). The base model has no
     implicit calibration for these unusual preference profiles — it defaults
     to population-average aesthetics. RAG corrects this by injecting a handful
     of concrete ratings from that same demographic segment in the training
     data, giving the model an anchor.

  b) CULTURALLY-SPECIFIC AESTHETIC PREFERENCES: Personas 22 (Orthodox rabbi)
     and 19 (Latina teacher) have aesthetic preferences shaped by cultural or
     community identity rather than general demographic markers. A CLIP-based
     retrieval that finds visually similar sites and associates them with the
     persona's actual ratings fills in this cultural specificity that the system
     prompt alone cannot convey.

  c) LOW BASELINE = HIGH HEADROOM: When the baseline model is barely above
     zero correlation, even small absolute improvements from RAG appear as
     enormous percentage gains. The model corrects a near-random initial
     response into a weakly aligned one, which counts as massive relative
     improvement.

  - Persona 7  (White man, rural Louisiana, retired Baptist)            :  -81%
                0.038 → 0.007
  - Persona 4  (White woman, Indiana, Mormon homeschooling mother)      :  -51%

  - Persona 37 (Hispanic woman, Phoenix, real estate, moderate)         :  -48%
                0.302 → 0.158
  - Persona 31 (White man, retired judge, moderate Independent)         :  -45%
                0.084 → 0.047
  - Persona 30 (Hispanic woman, NM, elderly, Catholic, low-income)     :  -36%
                0.221 → 0.142
  - Persona 17 (White woman, Vermont, retired professor, very liberal)  :  -24%
                0.252 → 0.192

Why does RAG hurt these personas?

  a) DEMOGRAPHIC CONTAMINATION IN RETRIEVAL: The CLIP embedding captures
     visual similarity but knows nothing about the persona's demographics.
     When the top-k retrieved training examples come from a different age,
     income, or education group than the target persona, the in-context
     ratings introduce conflicting signals. This is most damaging for
     demographically isolated personas with no strong visual cluster.

     Persona 33 (wealthy Black moderate) is a clear example: the model may
     retrieve examples from demographically similar training instances (other
     Black respondents) who hold very different aesthetic preferences due to
     different socioeconomic status — actively pointing the model in the
     wrong direction and flipping the correlation to negative.

  b) MODERATE PERSONAS HAVE HETEROGENEOUS PREFERENCES: Personas 31, 33, 37
     are political moderates. Moderates by definition aggregate diverse
     sub-preferences, making their average aesthetic ratings less predictable
     from any single demographic cluster. Retrieved examples from near
     neighbours may emphasise one sub-tendency (e.g., conservative aesthetics)
     when the persona's actual preference is a blend. The baseline model,
     lacking any examples, produces less biased average predictions that
     correlate better with the moderate's averaged-out ratings.

  c) ELDERLY, LOW-EDUCATION PERSONAS: Personas 7, 30 (aged 67-75, minimal
     education) may have aesthetic preferences less tied to recognisable visual
     patterns. If retrieved training examples come from younger or more
     educated raters of superficially similar sites, the injected ratings are
     systematically off, degrading the model's accuracy.

  d) HIGH-BASELINE PERSONAS HAVE LESS ROOM AND MORE TO LOSE: Personas 37
     (r_baseline=0.302) and 17 (r_baseline=0.252) were already reasonably
     well-calibrated. For these personas the RAG examples either introduce
     noise that disrupts a good signal, or the retrieved similar images happen
     to be the ones the persona rates idiosyncratically, leading the model
     astray on exactly the outlier cases.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4. CROSS-CUTTING PATTERNS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A. Low Baseline → RAG Helps (consistently)
   Personas with r_baseline < 0.10 (6, 7, 25, 31) all received meaningful
   RAG intervention. Of these four, three (6, 22, 25) improved substantially.
   The exception, persona 7 (rural Louisiana Baptist), worsened further —
   suggesting the demographic retrieval mismatch problem is severe enough to
   override even the low-baseline headroom.

B. Very Liberal Personas — Mixed Results
   Very liberal personas split sharply:
     Improved: 11, 13, 15, 16, 18, 19, 20, 27 (8 personas)
     Degraded: 14, 17 (2 personas)
   Younger, lower-income, urban-liberal personas benefit from RAG; older,
   higher-income liberal personas (tech executive, retired professor) do not.
   This suggests the model struggles to distinguish within-ideology variation
   that is driven by socioeconomic class rather than ideology itself.

C. Very Conservative Personas — Mixed Results
   Similarly varied:
     Improved: 1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 8, 9, 21, 22, 23, 24, 25 (12 personas)
     Degraded: 4, 7, 10 (3 personas)
   The degraded conservative personas all share one trait: they are defined
   more by religious identity (Mormon, Southern Baptist) or extreme age/
   geographic isolation than by any visually identifiable lifestyle. Religion-
   centric aesthetic preferences are essentially invisible to CLIP, so the
   retrieved examples are driven by visual content that correlates poorly
   with religious aesthetic values.

D. Persona Uniqueness Predicts RAG Benefit
   The strongest RAG improvements come from personas whose combination of
   attributes is rare in the general population (Native American progressive,
   Black conservative in law enforcement, Orthodox rabbi). These are the exact
   cases where the base model most needs concrete examples to override its
   default population-mean behaviour, and where even a few well-matched
   training examples from the vector DB provide a strong directional signal.

E. The CLIP Retrieval Signal is Imperfect
   CLIP retrieves visually similar websites but cannot guarantee demographic
   alignment between the retrieved raters and the target persona. The ideal
   retrieval would be: "find websites that THIS persona has rated" — which is
   exactly what the per-persona vector DB does. However, when the training
   split for a persona is thin (few training examples, persona_32 had only 13),
   the retrieved examples may not fully represent the persona's preferences,
   limiting RAG effectiveness.
